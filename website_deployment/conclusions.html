Conclusions

With the R-Squared value of 99%, we can say that the data best fit the regression model. The adjusted R-squared penalizes the R-squared formula based on the number of variables; therefore, a higher adjusted score means most of the variables are contributing to our model's R-squared properly. After looking at the p values, some of the demographic features (Asian, black, Hispanic, native Americans, white), total cohort, regentsâ€™ data, and the number of English language learners are significant as they are less than 0.05. so, we can reject the null hypothesis that these features make a difference in the student dropout rate. Poverty features (poverty number, Economic Need Index) are not significant as they are higher than 0.05. so, we can conclude that these features make a difference in the student dropout rate.

In conclusion, we have discovered that finding a concrete reason for drop out rates is complicated. While poverty does play a part in predicted drop out rates, factors such as teacher qualifications, school funding, and student involvement can also play a part in overall drop out rates. These factors were not considered due to the fact that our team did not have the data for them.

For future analysis, some improvements that could be made would be to perform this analysis on multiple cities in America. Perhaps a more linear trend could be discovered if a plethora of city data on schools could be fed into the machine learning algorithm. Additionally, having an updated dataset would be of great help in making an official analysis.
